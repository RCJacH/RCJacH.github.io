Interval

April 4, 2019 · 245 words
Musicianship
Theory · Musicianship

Definition

Interval describes the relative distance between two notes that make sound simultaneously, continuously, or disjointedly.

Properties

  1. An interval is always measured from the lower sounding note to the higher sounding note, regardless of their chronological sequence of appearance.
  2. Intervals have two properties: quantity and quality. When combined, quality appears before quantity.
  3. Quantity is the measurement of distance between the alphabet of the two comparing notes, starting with the starting note as 1, adding 1 for each different alphabet (octave sensitive) it goes through after landing on the targeting note.
    • In English, quantity is named as positions with the exception of the first as unison and the eighth as octave.
  4. Quality marks the offset an intervalic quantity deviation from the major scale built upon the lower note. The naming scheme is as followed:
    • For interval with quantity of 2, 3, 6, or 7, when wrapped into an octave, if the pitch name equals to the same degree from the major scale, the quality of the interval is a major one. Those with a semitone lower from the major scale is called a minor interval. For the rest of the quantities - 1, 4, 5, and 8 with their equivalence beyond one octave - the quality matching that of the major scale is called a perfect interval.
    • Extending the range of the quality from above, intervals with a semitone higher than the norm is named augmented, and diminished for the lower counterpart.
